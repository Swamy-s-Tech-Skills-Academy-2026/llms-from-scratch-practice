# My Reading Strategy Notes (Chapter 1)

**Date**: February 21, 2026

Chapter 1 has no code — it's purely conceptual. But I need a reading strategy so I don't just passively skim chapters without absorbing anything. I'm writing this down so I actually stick to it.

---

## What I'm trying to solve

I have a bad habit: I read technical books like novels, feel like I "got it", then realize I can't explain anything a week later. I want to fix that with this book.

---

## My approach (5 passes per chapter)

### Pass 1 — Big picture read (offline, no computer)

Read the chapter straight through without writing any code. The goal is just the overall picture. I highlight things that confuse me or seem important. I do NOT look things up at this stage.

**Why**: Jumping to code too early makes me lose the thread. I need the conceptual scaffolding first.

### Pass 2 — Code along (retype, don't copy)

On the second pass I open my notebook and **retype** the code from scratch. Not copy-paste — actually type it out. When I get different results than expected, I dig into why before moving on.

**My takeaway**: Typing forces me to think about each line. Copying makes me feel productive while learning nothing.

### Pass 3 — Exercises

After running the code, I attempt the exercises without peeking at solutions. If I'm truly stuck after a genuine attempt, I look at the solution and make sure I understand it before moving on.

### Pass 4 — Review and clarify

I revisit my highlights from Pass 1 and my confusions from Pass 2. I look things up, find extra references, or just sit with the question until it clicks.

**I usually need to clarify**: math notation, why a particular design choice was made, and what happens if I break something intentionally.

### Pass 5 — Use it in a project

Eventually I want to apply what I learned. This could be a small experiment, a variant of the chapter code, or building something new on top of what I know.

---

## Why not skip chapters?

I'm tempted to jump straight to attention (Chapter 3) or training (Chapter 4). But each chapter builds directly on the previous one — skipping tokenization means I won't understand why embedding dimensions matter, and so on. I need to respect the order.

---

## What "done" looks like for a chapter

I consider a chapter complete when I can:

- Explain the main idea to someone without looking at notes
- Reproduce the key code blocks from memory (not perfectly, but structurally)
- Answer: "What would break if I changed X?"
